· Hs 91; 680
Berlin, March 16, 1928.

Dear Professor,

Many thanks for your card and for the galley proofs of your fine book.

Without having familiarised myself exhaustively with it, I should like to make a brief remark concerning your treatment of the symmetric group.

On p. 130, § 52, you prove Frobenius’s lemma on the elements of \( \Sigma P \), and then on p. 131 the further lemma

\[ \Sigma(c\sigma g t) \;=\; c\sigma g\,\Sigma t . \]

If on p. 131, according to your statements,

\[ \sum_{p} c(cspt) \;\ge\; c(cs)\,\sum_{p} c(pt) \]

on which everything else depends and which — together with its later application — brings rather involved calculations into the discussion, would it not be clearer and simpler to proceed here as follows:

Lemma 1. As with you on p. 130.

Lemma 2. The conditions

\(x(S\,p)=x(S),\; \)\(x(I_{95})=\delta_{9}\,x(S)\)

(\(p,q\in P\) or respectively \(\mathbb Z\)) are satisfied only by the group numbers

\[ \chi(S)=\text{constant}\,\cdot c(S) . \]

Proof: Necessity is obvious.

Sufficiency is shown as follows: if \(x^{I}(s)\) satisfies the condition, then for the elements of \(PQ\) one sees at once

\[ x^{I}(s)=x^{I}(0)\,c(s)=\text{constant}\,\cdot c(s). \]

If \(s\notin PQ\), choose \(p,q\) with

\[ sps^{-1}=q,\;\; \text{i. e. } qsp=s , \]

(Lemma 1.), then

\[ x(s)=x(qsp)=-\,x(s1)\;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; x(s)=0, \]

Q.E.D.

Lemma 3. For every group number x one has

\[ c \times c \;=\; \text{constant}\, c . \]

Proof: for together with \(c\) the function \(c \times c\) clearly also satisfies the condition in Lemma 2.

From this it now follows

I. \(c^{2}=\gamma c\), then \(\gamma>0\) (as with you), hence \(\dfrac{1}{c}\) is a characteristic unit.

II. were c not primitive, then …

Further, let

\[ jc = a + b,\quad a^{2}=a,\; b^{2}=b,\; ab=ba=0, \]

then

\[ a = (a + b)\,a\,(a + b) = jc\,c\,a\,c = \text{constant}\; jc, \]

and because \(a^{2}=a\) it follows that constant = 0 or 1, i. e.

either \(a = jc,\; b = 0,\) or \(a = 0,\; b = jc\).

Hence \(jc\) is primitive.

… five unpublished.

I believe this method is excellent for the more computational problems. Admittedly you obtain the affine group at the same time, but as far as I can see this arrangement nevertheless represents a shortcut. What do you think of it?

With best regards I remain

Yours faithfully,

J. v. Neumann.
