Hs. 91; 680
Berlin, July 16, 1928.

Dear Professor!

Many thanks for your postcard, and the proofs of your fine little book.

Without having made myself thoroughly familiar with them, I would like to make a brief remark about your treatment of the symmetric group.

On p. 130, § 52, you prove the Frobenius lemma about the elements of \( \mathcal{QB} \), and then on p. 131 the further lemma

\[ \sum_{p} c(cspt) = c(cs)\, \sum_{p} c(pt) \]

on which everything else depends, and which (as well as its later "exploitation") brings considerable calculations into the arguments.

Would it not be more transparent and simpler to proceed as follows:

Lemma 1. As in your treatment on p. 130

Lemma 2. The conditions

\(x(sp)=x(s), \quad x(qs)=\delta_q x(s)\)

(p, q entirely from \( \mathcal{B} \) or \(\mathcal{Q}\) respectively) are satisfied only by the group numbers

\(x(s)=\text{Constant} \cdot \;c(s)\)

Proof: The necessity is clear. The sufficiency is shown as follows: if \(x(s)\) satisfies the condition, then one immediately sees for the \(s\) from \(\mathcal{B}\;\mathcal{Q}\)

\(x(s)=x(0)\cdot c(s)=\text{Constant} \cdot \;c(s)\)

If s does not lie in \(\mathcal{BQ}\), then let p, q be chosen with

\(sps^{-1}=q\), i.e. \(qsp=s\)

(Lemma 1.), then

\(x(s)=x(qsp)=-x(s),\ x(s)=0.\)

q.e.d.

Lemma 3. For every group number x one has

\(c \times c = \text{Constant }\cdot c\)

Proof: For with c, obviously \(c \times c\) also satisfies the condition in Lemma 2.

From this now follows

I. \(c^2 : \gamma c\), then \( \gamma > 0 \) as in your treatment, thus \(\frac{1}{\gamma} c\) is a characteristic unit.

II. From

\(\frac{1}{\gamma}c = a + b,\quad a^2 = a,\quad b^2 = b,\quad ab = ba = 0\)

follows

\(a = (a+b)\,a\,(a+b) = \frac{1}{\gamma^2}cac = \text{Constant }\frac{1}{\gamma}c\)

thus because of \(a^{2}=a\) Constant = 0, 1, i.e., either \(a = \frac{1}{\gamma}c, \; b = 0\), or \(a = 0, \; b = \frac{1}{\gamma}c\),

Therefore \(\frac{1}{\gamma}c\) is primitive, and the representation irreducible.

I believe that this method is preferable to the much more computational approach. Of course they simultaneously obtain the affine group, but as far as I can see this arrangement still represents a simplification. What do you think of it?

I remain with best regards

Yours truly

J. v. Neumann.
